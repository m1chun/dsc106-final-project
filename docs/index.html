Where California Burns: A Geographic Story of Wildfire Risk

David Goldstein, Margot Nissen, and Mieko Chun

Project Overview

We like the idea of modeling fires like we did in Project 3, but wanted to work with different data more relevant to California. Cal-Fire provides excellent interactive datasets in CSV and GeoJSON formats.

Our goal is to determine which ecosystems are more fire-resistant by examining both fire-prone regions and severity patterns.

California Seed Zones

California is divided into 85 seed zones based on climate and terrain. These determine plant adaptability and fire behavior.

California’s Major Physiographic & Climatic Regions

To better understand wildfire behavior, California can be divided into six broad physiographic and climatic regions. Each region has distinct vegetation, moisture, temperature, and wind patterns that strongly shape fire risk.

North Coast Redwood (090 Series)

This narrow fog belt stretches from southern Oregon to Monterey Bay. Constant coastal moisture supports dense redwood forests with extremely high biomass.

  • Fog-dominated climate
  • Cool, moist conditions
  • Low natural ignition but extreme fuel loads

Central Coast (100 Series)

This region extends from Monterey Bay to Santa Barbara and includes the Channel Islands. Mediterranean climate dominates with wet winters and dry summers.

  • Chaparral and oak woodland
  • High coastal wind exposure
  • Frequent human-caused ignitions

North Coast Interior (300 Series)

Located between the coastal fog belt and the Sacramento Valley, this region is warmer and drier than the coast but still heavily forested.

  • Dense mixed conifer forests
  • Hotter summers than coastal zones
  • High lightning ignition risk

West Slope Cascades–Sierra (500 Series)

This expansive region runs along the western face of the Sierra Nevada and Cascades. It is one of California’s most fire-active forest belts.

  • Heavy winter snowpack → dry summer fuels
  • Frequent lightning storms
  • Large, fast-spreading crown fires

East Slope Cascades–Sierra (700 Series)

The eastern slope lies in a rain shadow, producing much drier conditions than the west. Vegetation is sparse but highly flammable.

  • Dry mountain climate
  • Strong downslope winds
  • Flashy, fast-moving fires

The Four Catchall Regions (900 Series)

This group contains four highly distinct landscapes that behave very differently during wildfire events.

  • 950: Great Basin ( northeast California desert grasslands )
  • 960: Central Valley ( agricultural lowlands )
  • 980: Southern California Desert ( Mojave + Colorado )
  • 990: Southern California Mountains ( conifer forests )
